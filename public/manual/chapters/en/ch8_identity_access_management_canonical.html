Objective 4.6 — Identity & Access Management

Identity and Access Management (IAM) is the discipline that governs who can access which resources, how their identity is verified, and which actions they are allowed to perform after authentication. It is not simply password and login management: IAM covers the full identity lifecycle, from initial creation and verification, through granular privilege assignment, to complete revocation when the subject no longer has a right to access.

Architecturally, IAM connects the Zero Trust model, which requires continuous verification, network protocols that transport credentials, and organizational policies that define what is allowed. The SY0-701 exam tests whether you can choose the correct access mechanism for a scenario: service accounts, federated users, temporary privileged access, and biometric authentication require very different technical answers.

Identity and attributes

An identity is the structured set of claims that describe a subject inside a system. The subject may be a person, an application, a device, a service, or an entire organization. Identity is not the same as an account: the account is the technical representation of the identity in a system, while identity is the broader concept that includes attributes, roles, organizational relationships, and action history.

Attributes associated with an identity fall into two categories: attributes that change over time, such as role, department, or clearance level, and stable intrinsic traits, such as fingerprints or facial geometry. This distinction matters because stronger authentication mechanisms combine both types: they verify something the subject has and something the subject is.

Subject
Entity requesting access to a resource. It may be human, software, such as a process or microservice, or hardware, such as an IoT device. In Zero Trust, every subject is considered untrusted by default and must prove its identity and security posture before receiving access.
Claim / Assertion
Statement about a subject's identity produced by an authentication system or identity provider. For example, “this user belongs to the Administrators group” is a claim. Claims are carried by protocols such as SAML and OAuth and are verified by the service provider before access is granted.
Identity Provider (IdP)
System or organization that authenticates the subject and produces identity assertions. In a federated model, the IdP is the central trust point: if a service provider trusts the IdP, it accepts its assertions without requiring separate local authentication. Examples include Microsoft Entra ID, Okta, and Google Workspace.
Service Provider (SP)
Application or service that delegates authentication to an external IdP. The SP receives assertions from the IdP, verifies their cryptographic signature, and grants access according to local policies. The SP does not manage its own credentials; it relies on the trust chain with the IdP.
Relying Party (RP)
Term used in OpenID Connect and OAuth for the application that relies on the identity provider to verify identity. Functionally, it is similar to the Service Provider in SAML, but the term is used in different protocol ecosystems.

Account provisioning and de-provisioning

Provisioning is the formal process used to create a user account, assign roles and permissions, and connect it to the resources required for the user’s duties. Correct provisioning follows the principle of least privilege: only the permissions strictly required for the role are assigned. Provisioning is not a one-time event; it must be reviewed every time a person’s duties change.

De-provisioning is equally critical from a security perspective. When an employee leaves the organization or changes role, all access that is no longer justified must be revoked promptly. Orphaned accounts, meaning active accounts belonging to former employees or retired systems, are a common weakness in corporate breaches because their credentials are rarely monitored and may remain valid for months.

Practical example — Joiner/Mover/Leaver:
A company uses the Joiner-Mover-Leaver framework to manage the account lifecycle. When someone joins, provisioning is automated through the HR system into Active Directory. When the person changes department, old-role permissions are revoked and new-role permissions are assigned within 24 hours. When the person leaves, the account is disabled on the same day and deleted after 30 days of retention.
Account lifecycle
The set of phases an account goes through: creation → activation → permission changes for promotions or transfers → temporary suspension for extended leave or disciplinary processes → disabling → final deletion. Strong IAM governance automates these transitions as much as possible to reduce human error.
Orphaned account
An account that remains active after the subject it belongs to no longer has a right to access. Common causes include failed offboarding or lack of integration between HR and identity management. Orphaned accounts are prime targets for credential stuffing and privilege escalation.
Permission creep
Progressive accumulation of permissions over time caused by missing revocation during role changes. A user who has changed position three times may end up with permissions from all previous roles, violating least privilege. It is mitigated with periodic access reviews or user access reviews.
EXAM TIP 4.6 — Provisioning: in questions about orphaned accounts or permission creep, the correct answer usually points to periodic access review or automated integration between HR and identity management. If the scenario says a former employee can still access systems, the cause is failed de-provisioning and the solution is immediate account disabling plus access review.

Identity Proofing

Identity proofing is the process by which an organization verifies that the person requesting an account or access really is who they claim to be, before credentials are created. It is not authentication, which occurs later, but a one-time verification of the requester’s real identity. NIST SP 800-63A defines three identity proofing assurance levels: IAL1 (self-assertion), IAL2 (remotely verified documents), and IAL3 (physical verification with biometrics).

In corporate environments, identity proofing typically occurs during onboarding: HR verifies ID documents, contract, and references before IT creates the account. In digital services, such as online bank account opening, identity proofing may use document OCR, liveness detection, and checks against public databases.

EXAM TIP 4.6 — Identity proofing: if the scenario describes the moment when the account is created, it is identity proofing. If it describes the moment when an existing account is used to log in, it is authentication. The timing is the key discriminator.

Attestation

Attestation is the formal process in which an authorized person, typically a manager, data owner, or IT owner, periodically certifies that the access assigned to an account is still appropriate and necessary. Attestation differs from identity proofing: identity proofing happens once at account creation, while attestation is a recurring process that combats permission creep.

Technically, attestation creates a formal audit trail: who certified that access was legitimate, when, and in what context. This is essential for compliance with frameworks and regulations such as SOX, HIPAA, and ISO 27001, which require documentary evidence that privileges have been reviewed and approved regularly.

PITFALL — Attestation vs Auditing: auditing automatically records actions performed, such as who did what and when, without human judgment. Attestation is a deliberate human review of existing access to confirm that it remains appropriate. If the scenario says someone certifies permissions, choose attestation; if it mentions automatic logs, choose auditing.

Permission assignments and implications

Permission assignment defines which actions a subject may perform on a resource. Its implications are often underestimated: overly broad permissions not only violate least privilege but create a real attack surface. A user with write permission on a network share can delete critical files, deliberately or accidentally; a service account with Domain Admin rights can be abused to escalate across the entire infrastructure.

EXAM TIP 4.6 — Permission implications: when a scenario describes a service account, application, or automated system, the correct answer almost always assigns only the minimum necessary permissions for that function. If the backup service account only needs to read files, it should not have Write or Execute.

Multifactor Authentication (MFA)

Multifactor Authentication (MFA) requires two or more authentication factors from different categories. The core principle is that an attacker who compromises one factor, such as a password, cannot automatically compromise the other factors, such as a physical phone. MFA does not eliminate all risk, because MFA fatigue, SIM swapping, and real-time phishing proxies may bypass it, but it significantly raises the cost of attack.

For something to qualify as MFA, the factors must come from different categories. Two different passwords are two-step verification, not MFA. A password, which is something you know, plus an OTP from an authenticator app, which is something you have, is true MFA.

Authentication factors

Something you know (Knowledge)
Secret information known only to the subject: password, PIN, passphrase, or answer to a security question. It is the most common factor and also the most vulnerable: it can be phished, guessed, brute-forced, or revealed through social engineering.
Something you have (Possession)
A physical or digital object in the subject’s possession: smartphone with authenticator app, smart card, FIDO2 security key, or hardware OTP token. The assumption is that a remote attacker cannot possess the physical device. Residual risks include SIM swapping, device theft, and smart card duplication.
Something you are (Inherence / Biometrics)
A biometric characteristic of the subject: fingerprint, facial recognition, iris scan, voice, hand geometry, or typing rhythm. Advantages: it cannot be forgotten or lost. Disadvantages: it cannot be changed if compromised, may produce false positives or false negatives, requires dedicated hardware, and raises privacy concerns.
Somewhere you are (Geographic context)
The subject’s geographic or network location: IP address, GPS geolocation, recognized Wi-Fi network, or Bluetooth proximity. It is usually used as an additional context signal or as a trigger for step-up authentication. It is not strong on its own because location can be manipulated with VPNs.
PITFALL — MFA vs Two-Step: the exam makes a clear distinction. Two consecutive passwords = two-step, one factor type. Password + SMS OTP = MFA if the SMS is tied to the physical phone. Password + security question = two knowledge factors, therefore NOT MFA.

MFA implementations

Biometrics
Physiological or behavioral measurements used to verify identity. Key exam metrics are FAR (False Acceptance Rate), the percentage of impostors incorrectly accepted; FRR (False Rejection Rate), the percentage of legitimate users incorrectly rejected; and CER / EER (Crossover/Equal Error Rate), the point where FAR = FRR. A lower CER indicates a more accurate biometric system.
Hard authentication tokens
Dedicated physical devices that generate OTPs or sign cryptographic challenges. TOTP tokens generate a six-digit code that changes about every 30 seconds by synchronizing a shared secret with time. HOTP tokens use an incrementing counter. Hardware examples include RSA SecurID and YubiKey in OTP mode.
Soft authentication tokens
Smartphone apps that implement TOTP or HOTP, such as Google Authenticator, Microsoft Authenticator, and Authy. They avoid extra hardware but remain vulnerable if the device is compromised or if a real-time phishing proxy immediately reuses the OTP.
Security keys (FIDO2 / WebAuthn)
Hardware devices, such as YubiKey or Google Titan, or platform authenticators, such as Windows Hello or Face ID, based on FIDO2. They use public-key cryptography: the private key never leaves the device, and the token responds only for the exact domain where it was registered. They are among the strongest MFA methods available.
Practical example — MFA fatigue attack:
An attacker has stolen a user’s password, but the account uses push-notification MFA. The attacker sends dozens of push requests, hoping the tired or confused user will approve one. Countermeasure: require number matching, where the user must type a numeric code into the app instead of simply tapping “Approve.”
EXAM TIP 4.6 — MFA implementations: if the scenario asks for the MFA method most resistant to phishing, the answer is FIDO2/security key. Time-based OTP is TOTP; counter-based OTP is HOTP. Low CER = more accurate biometrics.

Password concepts

Passwords remain the most common authentication mechanism despite their known limitations. The key to protecting password-based systems is not imposing rigid and counterproductive rules, such as mandatory monthly changes, but applying evidence-based principles that balance security and usability. NIST SP 800-63B is the reference guidance and has overturned many traditional practices.

Password best practices

Length
Length is the single most important factor for brute-force resistance. A random 16-character password is exponentially harder to crack than a complex 8-character one. NIST recommends a minimum of 8 characters, 12–16+ as a best practice, and support for passphrases up to 64 characters.
Complexity
Using uppercase letters, lowercase letters, numbers, and symbols increases keyspace, but not as much as length. Complexity rules often push users toward predictable patterns: Password1! meets many requirements but is easy to crack. NIST recommends blocking known breached or dictionary passwords rather than imposing arbitrary complexity rules.
Reuse
Reusing the same password across systems is one of the most common compromise vectors. If a third-party site is breached, attackers try the same credentials against higher-value services. Systems should check new passwords against password history and prevent reuse.
Expiration
Mandatory periodic expiration was once standard, but NIST SP 800-63B challenged it because it drives users to weak predictable changes, such as Password1 → Password2. Current guidance recommends forcing a change only when there is evidence of compromise, not at arbitrary fixed intervals.
Age
Minimum password age prevents a user from changing a password again immediately after changing it. It stops users from cycling through password history quickly and then returning to the original password.
Password managers
Software that generates, stores, and autofills unique complex passwords for each service. They eliminate reuse and make long random passwords practical. The only password the user must remember is the vault master password, which must be strong and protected with MFA.
Practical example — NIST-based password policy:
An organization updates its policy by removing mandatory quarterly expiration, removing symbol requirements, checking new passwords against common breached passwords, requiring at least 12 characters, and enabling passphrases. Users choose longer passwords and stop making predictable expired-password variations.
PITFALL — Periodic expiration vs NIST: many exam questions compare traditional practice with modern NIST guidance. According to SY0-701 alignment, mandatory periodic expiration is not recommended unless there is evidence of compromise.

Passwordless

Passwordless authentication removes the traditional knowledge factor, the password, in favor of possession or inherence-based methods. Its main advantage is that there are no text credentials to steal through phishing or a data breach. Common implementations include FIDO2/WebAuthn with security keys or platform biometrics, magic links, push notifications with number matching, and client certificates.

In the passkey model, a FIDO2 consumer implementation, the private key is bound to the device and protected by local biometrics. Authentication uses a cryptographic challenge: the server sends a challenge, the device signs it with the private key, and the server verifies the signature with the public key. No shared secret travels over the network, so there is nothing useful to steal from the server side.

EXAM TIP 4.6 — Passwordless: if a scenario asks which authentication method removes credential-phishing risk, the answer is FIDO2/passkey-based passwordless. If it asks which method uses public-key cryptography for passwordless authentication, the answer is FIDO2/WebAuthn or client certificates.

Federation

Identity federation is a model in which multiple organizations or systems agree to trust one another’s authentication. Instead of every organization managing separate credentials for every user, a trust relationship is established between an identity provider and a service provider, allowing identities to be reused across organizational boundaries.

Federation is the technological foundation of modern B2B access. It allows an employee of Company A to access Company B applications using Company A credentials, without creating a separate Company B account. This simplifies access management but requires precise technical and governance agreements to define the trust chain.

Trust relationship
Formal agreement in which one entity, the service provider, accepts identity assertions produced by another entity, the identity provider, as sufficient to grant access. Trust is typically established through SAML metadata exchange or OAuth/OIDC configuration, with cryptographic verification of signed assertions.
Circle of trust
Group of service providers that all accept assertions from the same identity provider, forming a shared trust perimeter. Common in university consortia or enterprise cloud platforms.
Cross-domain identity management (SCIM)
Standard for automatic identity provisioning across different systems. It synchronizes accounts from HR or an IdP to federated service providers, so user creation or disabling propagates automatically through SCIM.
Practical example — B2B federation:
A consulting company works with a banking client. Instead of creating bank accounts for every consultant, the companies configure SAML federation: the consulting company is the IdP and the bank is the SP. Consultants authenticate with their company credentials and receive access to the bank’s systems through a SAML assertion. When a consultant leaves the consulting company, disabling the account in the IdP automatically removes access to the bank.

Single Sign-On (SSO)

Single Sign-On allows a user to authenticate once and access multiple applications and services without re-entering credentials. SSO reduces cognitive load, improves usability, and centralizes control: de-provisioning happens in one place and affects all connected services.

From a security perspective, SSO is a trade-off: it centralizes trust. If the IdP is compromised, all federated services are at risk. For this reason, the IdP must be protected with strong MFA, advanced monitoring, and strict privileged access controls.

LDAP (Lightweight Directory Access Protocol)

LDAP is a standard protocol for accessing and managing distributed directory information, typically used to store and query information about users, groups, devices, and policies in an enterprise network. The standard port is 389 for unencrypted LDAP and 636 for LDAPS, LDAP over TLS.

In a typical implementation, Microsoft Active Directory uses LDAP as the directory access protocol. Applications query a Domain Controller through LDAP to verify credentials and retrieve attributes such as groups, roles, and email. LDAP data is hierarchical: the DN, or Distinguished Name, uniquely identifies each object in the directory.

PITFALL — LDAP vs Kerberos in AD: in Active Directory, authentication is handled by Kerberos, not LDAP. LDAP is used for directory queries. Scenarios mentioning tickets and KDC refer to Kerberos; scenarios mentioning directory query or bind refer to LDAP.

SAML (Security Assertions Markup Language)

SAML is an XML-based standard for exchanging authentication and authorization data between an Identity Provider and a Service Provider. SAML 2.0 is the dominant current version for enterprise applications and web-based SSO.

The most common flow is the SP-initiated flow: the user accesses a service, the SP redirects the browser to the IdP, the user authenticates, the IdP generates a digitally signed SAML Assertion and sends it to the SP through the browser, and the SP verifies the signature, extracts user information, and grants access. The assertion contains attributes such as username, groups, and roles.

SAML componentRoleSimplified analogy
SAML AssertionSigned XML document proving the user’s identityThe “pass” signed by the IdP
Identity Provider (IdP)Authenticates the user and produces the assertionThe office that issues the pass

EXAM TIP 4.6 — SAML: SAML is the correct answer for scenarios describing enterprise or B2B SSO with XML assertions. If the scenario describes a mobile app or API delegating authorization without sharing credentials, the answer tends to be OAuth/OIDC.

OAuth and OpenID Connect (OIDC)

OAuth 2.0 is a delegated authorization framework: it allows an application to access protected resources on behalf of a user without the application seeing the user’s credentials. The central mechanism is the access token: the user authorizes the app, the authorization server issues a token, and the app uses that token to access APIs.

OpenID Connect (OIDC) is an authentication layer built on top of OAuth 2.0. It adds the ID token, usually a JWT, containing information about the user’s identity. OIDC is now the preferred solution for federated authentication in modern web and mobile applications.

PITFALL — OAuth is not authentication: OAuth 2.0 by itself is an authorization framework, not an authentication protocol. If you need to know who the user is, use OIDC. If you need to authorize an app to access resources, use OAuth. SAML can cover both in the same protocol.

Interoperability

Interoperability in IAM is the ability of different identity systems to work together, even when they belong to different organizations or technology platforms. Open standards such as SAML, OAuth, OIDC, and SCIM exist to make this possible: an Okta-based IdP must be able to federate with a Salesforce SP regardless of vendor.

Interoperability challenges usually appear in mergers, cloud migrations, or digital supply chains involving partners with different technologies. The solution is based on open standards and shared protocols, not proprietary vendor-specific mechanisms.

Access control models

An access control model defines the rules used to decide who may access what and under which conditions. The choice has deep architectural implications: it determines system flexibility, administrative cost, auditability, and resistance to insider attacks. Models are not mutually exclusive: an organization may use RBAC for enterprise apps, MAC for classified systems, and ABAC for cloud microservices.

MAC — Mandatory Access Control

In Mandatory Access Control, access decisions are determined by centrally enforced system policy, not by the resource owner. Each subject and object receives a security label or classification level. Access is allowed only when the subject’s clearance is compatible with the object’s classification under rigid rules the user cannot change.

MAC is used in government and military environments with classified data. Its advantage is resistance to human error and insider abuse: even a file owner cannot grant access to users with insufficient clearance. Its disadvantage is rigidity and high administrative cost.

DAC — Discretionary Access Control

In Discretionary Access Control, the resource owner decides who may access the resource and with which permissions. It is the model used by traditional filesystems: the creator of a file can modify permissions and share it at their discretion. DAC is flexible and intuitive, but if an authorized user is compromised or malicious, they may share resources with unauthorized users.

In Windows, NTFS file ACLs are an example of DAC. This also explains why malware running with a user’s privileges can read and encrypt all files the user owns: the system gives the owner control.

RBAC — Role-Based Access Control

In Role-Based Access Control, permissions are assigned to roles, and users are assigned to roles. A role is a predefined set of permissions aligned to a business function, such as Accountant, Developer, or IT Administrator. RBAC greatly simplifies management in large organizations because updating a role updates every user assigned to that role.

RBAC structurally reduces permission creep: when a user changes role, the old role’s permissions can be revoked and the new ones applied. The challenge is granularity: roles that are too broad cause over-provisioning, while too many roles create role explosion.

Practical example — RBAC in a hospital:
A hospital defines roles such as Doctor, Nurse, Administrative Staff, and IT Technician. Doctors can access clinical records for patients in their department. Nurses can read records but not prescribe. Administrative staff can see demographic data but not clinical information. When a doctor moves into an administrative role, the role change automatically revokes clinical-record access.

Rule-Based Access Control

Rule-Based Access Control uses administrator-defined conditional rules to control access, independently of the specific subject identity. Rules typically follow an “IF condition THEN action” structure: if the request comes from an internal IP and the time is between 9:00 and 18:00, allow access; otherwise deny it. Firewall ACLs are a classic example.

Rule-based control differs from RBAC because conditions do not necessarily depend on the subject’s role; they may depend on request attributes such as time of day, source IP, protocol, or device state. It can coexist with RBAC in complex systems.

PITFALL — Rule-based vs RBAC: RBAC = permissions based on the user’s role. Rule-based = permissions based on conditional rules, such as time, IP, or protocol. Firewall ACLs and time conditions point to Rule-based; groups and user roles point to RBAC.

ABAC — Attribute-Based Access Control

Attribute-Based Access Control is the most flexible and granular model. Access decisions are made by evaluating a combination of attributes about the subject, the object, and the environment at request time. A subject with department=HR requesting a file with classification=HR-confidential from an environment with network=corporate-vpn may receive access. If any attribute does not match, access is denied.

ABAC is a foundation of next-generation Zero Trust and cloud-native IAM systems. It enables highly precise policies without creating a role for every possible combination of conditions. Its disadvantage is policy complexity and the risk of conflicting rules.

Time-of-Day Restrictions and Least Privilege

Time-of-day restrictions
Access control that limits access to systems and resources to specific time windows. A part-time employee account may be configured to log in only from 8:00 to 17:00 Monday to Friday. Any attempt outside those hours is automatically denied even with valid credentials. This reduces the attack window for compromised accounts.
Least privilege
Core principle stating that every subject must have only the permissions strictly required to perform its function, nothing more. It applies to users, service accounts, applications, and system processes. It is continuous, not one-time: permissions must be periodically reviewed and reduced when no longer needed. It is closely related to need-to-know.
EXAM TIP 4.6 — Least privilege: when the scenario describes minimizing permissions, choose least privilege. When it describes excessive permissions accumulated over time, identify permission creep and solve it with access review. When it describes access limited to certain hours, choose time-of-day restrictions.

Privileged Access Management (PAM)

Privileged Access Management is the set of tools, processes, and controls that govern the use of privileged accounts, meaning accounts with administrative power over systems, infrastructure, or critical data. Domain Admin, root, and DBA accounts are high-value targets because compromising them gives broad access to entire systems, not just individual files.

Modern PAM uses just enough access (JEA) combined with just-in-time (JIT): administrative privileges are not permanent but are granted only for the time required to perform a specific operation and then automatically revoked. This drastically reduces exposure time.

Just-in-Time (JIT) Permissions

Just-in-time permissions are temporary privileges granted only when needed and for the minimum necessary duration. Instead of having a permanent Domain Admin account, an administrator requests privilege elevation for a specific task, such as installing a patch, receives temporary access, for example one hour, performs the work, and the privileges are automatically revoked when the time expires or the task completes.

The security benefit is significant: an attacker who compromises the administrator’s account outside a JIT window gains no elevated privilege. Systems that implement JIT include Microsoft PIM in Entra ID, CyberArk, and HashiCorp Vault.

Password Vaulting

Password vaulting stores privileged credentials in a secure centralized vault instead of in configuration files, sticky notes, or administrator memory. The vault encrypts credentials at rest, controls who can retrieve them, records every access in an audit log, and can automatically rotate passwords after use.

A typical check-out workflow is: the administrator requests a system password from the vault; the vault verifies identity and permissions; it records the request; it provides the password for a limited time; at the end of the session it rotates the password automatically. This way, no administrator permanently knows the real password.

Practical example — Password vaulting for service accounts:
A web application must connect to a database. The DB password is not stored in the configuration file, where developers or repository attackers could read it, but retrieved at runtime from a vault such as HashiCorp Vault or AWS Secrets Manager. The vault verifies that the application may access that credential and returns a temporary token with a one-hour TTL. When it expires, the application requests a new one.

Ephemeral Credentials

Ephemeral credentials are credentials with a very short lifecycle, from seconds to hours, generated dynamically for a specific session or operation and automatically expired. They do not exist before being requested and no longer exist after expiration, eliminating the risk associated with long-lived static credentials.

They are a standard pattern in modern cloud systems: AWS STS generates temporary API credentials, typically valid from 15 minutes to 12 hours. Short-lived X.509 certificates for mutual TLS, JWTs with a short TTL, and HashiCorp Vault leases are all examples of ephemeral credentials.

EXAM TIP 4.6 — PAM tools: temporary administrator access with automatic revocation = just-in-time. Credentials stored in a secure system with rotation = password vaulting. Dynamically generated credentials expiring after minutes or seconds = ephemeral credentials. These concepts are complementary and often used together.

Summary flashcards — Chapter 8

  • Identity proofing — verifies the requester’s real identity before account creation; it happens once during onboarding.
  • Attestation — periodic formal review of existing access by an owner or manager to confirm it is still appropriate.
  • Provisioning / de-provisioning — formal creation and revocation of accounts following the identity lifecycle; timely de-provisioning prevents orphaned accounts.
  • Permission creep — gradual accumulation of permissions during role changes; prevented with periodic access reviews.
  • Something you know / have / are / somewhere — MFA factor categories; MFA requires at least two different categories.
  • TOTP — time-based OTP; HOTP — counter-based OTP.
  • FAR / FRR / CER — biometric metrics: FAR = impostors accepted, FRR = legitimate users rejected, CER = balance point; lower is better.
  • FIDO2 / WebAuthn — phishing-resistant passwordless standard; the private key never leaves the device and responds only to the registered domain.
  • SAML 2.0 — XML enterprise standard for federated SSO; produces signed assertions for B2B and enterprise web applications.
  • OAuth 2.0 — delegated authorization framework, not authentication; OIDC adds authentication on top of OAuth.
  • LDAP port 389 / LDAPS 636 — directory query protocol; Kerberos handles authentication, LDAP handles queries.
  • MAC — label classification + clearance level, decided by the system, not the user; used in government and military environments.
  • DAC — the owner decides permissions; flexible but risky if the owner account is compromised.
  • RBAC — permissions assigned to roles and users assigned to roles; structurally reduces permission creep.
  • ABAC — decision based on subject + object + environment attributes; more granular than RBAC and foundational for Zero Trust.
  • Just-in-time permissions — elevated privileges granted only for a specific task and then automatically revoked.
  • Password vaulting — privileged credentials stored in an encrypted vault with automatic rotation; no human permanently knows the password.
  • Ephemeral credentials — dynamically generated credentials with a very short TTL; reduce static credential theft risk.
