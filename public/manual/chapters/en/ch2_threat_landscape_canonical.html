Objective 2.1 — Threat actors and motivations

A threat actor is any entity — individual, collective, or state-sponsored — capable of taking actions with a negative impact on the confidentiality, integrity, or availability of a target's information systems. To defend effectively it is essential to understand who is attacking, with what resources, and why: the answer to these three questions guides the selection of appropriate security controls and the level of justified investment. The SY0-701 exam requires being able to classify an actor from a scenario and match it to the most likely motivation.

Threat actor types

Nation-state
A group of hackers sponsored by or directly controlled by a government. They have unlimited budgets, teams of specialists, and access to zero-day vulnerabilities developed or purchased specifically for this purpose. Typical targets are critical infrastructure (power grids, water systems), military systems, government agencies, and strategic companies. The distinguishing characteristic is persistence: a nation-state can remain hidden in a system for months or years (Advanced Persistent Threat — APT) before acting. Historically documented examples: attacks on nuclear power plants, theft of industrial plans from aerospace companies, electoral interference through disinformation campaigns.
Unskilled attacker (Script kiddie)
An actor with limited technical skills who uses pre-packaged tools, public exploits, and tutorials available online without necessarily understanding how they work. The term "script kiddie" derives from the fact that these actors run scripts written by others. They are not harmless for this reason: indiscriminate mass attacks, website defacement, and automated mass scans are often the work of this profile. Their danger is proportional to the spread of available automated tools.
Hacktivist
An activist who uses hacking techniques to support a political, social, or ideological cause. The objective is not financial gain but reputational damage, website defacement, publication of confidential data (doxing), or disruption of services of organizations considered adversaries. Hacktivist groups often act in a coordinated manner but without a formal structure, making attribution difficult. The philosophical/political motivation is what distinguishes them from other actors.
Insider threat
An employee, former employee, contractor, or anyone with legitimate access to the organization's systems who uses it in a harmful way. It is statistically the most costly threat because the insider already knows the internal architecture, policies, weak points, and — most importantly — has already passed perimeter controls. A distinction is made between malicious insiders (acting deliberately for harm or gain) and negligent insiders (causing unintentional damage, for example by opening a phishing attachment). Behavioral monitoring (User and Entity Behavior Analytics — UEBA) is the primary defense tool.
Organized crime
Structured criminal groups that operate with a business logic: they have a hierarchy, internal specializations (developers, money mules, spam campaign operators), clear financial objectives, and often offer services on the dark web (Ransomware-as-a-Service, phishing kits). Organized crime is responsible for the vast majority of ransomware attacks, banking fraud, and stolen credential markets. Unlike nation-states, their primary objective is rapid and measurable profit.
Shadow IT
Systems, applications, or cloud services adopted by employees or business departments without approval or oversight from the IT/security team. It is not an external actor in the strict sense, but represents an internal attack surface: a personal Dropbox account used to transfer company documents, an unapproved SaaS app that receives sensitive data, or an uncatalogued server active on an internal port. The problem is that these systems escape patch management processes, centralized access controls, and the organization's security logs.
Exam tip — 2.1 Threat actors: The exam often presents a brief scenario ("a disgruntled employee downloaded data before resigning" → malicious insider threat; "a group attacks pharmaceutical companies to publish their prices" → hacktivist; "an attack persists for 18 months on energy infrastructure" → nation-state/APT). Always match the actor to the motivation most consistent with the context of the scenario.

Actor attributes

Beyond type, each threat actor is characterized on three axes that determine how dangerous and difficult to detect they are:

Internal / External
An internal actor already has legitimate physical or logical access to the organization's network: they can operate without needing to breach the perimeter and their movements blend in with normal traffic. An external actor must instead overcome perimeter defenses (firewall, VPN, exposed portals) before being able to act. This distinction is fundamental because a malicious insider defeats most perimeter security controls.
Resources / Funding
Available resources determine the quality and quantity of tools used. A script kiddie uses free tools and rented botnets; organized crime has budget to purchase zero-days and hire specialists; a nation-state can fund teams of hundreds of full-time researchers. High resources translate into more sophisticated, more persistent, and harder-to-attribute attacks.
Level of sophistication / Capability
Measures the actor's technical capability: can they develop custom malware? Do they know EDR evasion techniques? Do they have access to proprietary zero-days? A low level of sophistication means opportunistic and noisy attacks (easy to detect); a high level means targeted, silent attacks difficult to distinguish from legitimate activity. Nation-states are generally the most sophisticated actors.

Motivations

Understanding an actor's motivation allows anticipating probable targets and the type of expected damage. Objective 2.1 explicitly lists the following motivations:

Data exfiltration
The objective is to steal data — credentials, intellectual property, personal data, trade secrets — to resell, use as leverage, or to gain a competitive advantage. It is the most common motivation by far and spans almost all actors (nation-state, organized crime, malicious insider).
Espionage
Systematic and silent collection of information about governments, companies, or individuals for the benefit of a competitor or state. Differs from generic exfiltration by focusing on long-term strategic intelligence. Typically associated with nation-states and actors with APT capabilities.
Service disruption
The objective is not to steal data but to make systems and services inaccessible. The typical vector is DDoS (Distributed Denial of Service) or ransomware that locks systems. Motivations: retaliation (hacktivist), extortion (organized crime), strategic sabotage (nation-state against critical infrastructure).
Blackmail
The actor acquires compromising data (private photos, confidential communications, company secrets) and threatens to publish them unless paid. Common techniques: ransomware with double extortion (first encrypts data, then threatens to publish it), sextortion, theft of sensitive company emails. Typical of organized crime.
Financial gain
The most widespread motivation in cybercrime. Includes ransomware, banking fraud, credit card theft, unauthorized cryptomining, BEC (Business Email Compromise), and any scheme that converts the attack into money directly or indirectly.
Philosophical / Political beliefs
Typical motivation of hacktivists: the attack is an act of protest or a means to amplify a message. Targets are organizations perceived as enemies of the cause (governments, multinationals, religious entities). Economic gain is absent or secondary; reputational damage and public visibility are the primary objectives.
Ethical
An actor who violates systems convinced of acting for the common good: exposing vulnerabilities on public systems, publishing data from corrupt entities, or conducting unauthorized tests to "demonstrate" a problem. Even with declared good intentions, this still constitutes illegal activity. Must be distinguished from a bug bounty hunter operating within authorized programs.
Revenge
Typical motivation of the malicious insider or former employee: responding to a perceived wrong (dismissal, missed promotion, conflict with management). The attack is targeted and intentional, and the person executing it already knows the system. It is one of the most dangerous categories because it combines internal knowledge with strong motivation.
Disruption / Chaos
Some actors have no specific objective: they simply want to create disorder, demonstrate that a system is vulnerable, or test their own capabilities. Self-reproducing worms launched without control or attacks causing unforeseen collateral damage fall into this category.
War
Cyber-warfare is now a permanent component of modern conflicts. A state uses its offensive capabilities to sabotage enemy infrastructure (power grids, command systems), collect military intelligence, and influence public opinion in the adversary country. The distinction between espionage and cyber warfare is often thin and depends on the impact of the actions taken.
Practical example — matching actor and motivation: A pharmaceutical company suffers an unauthorized access that remains silent for 14 months. Logs show periodic exfiltration of research files on formulas for new drugs. No ransom is demanded. Profile: nation-state with motivation espionage / data exfiltration. The long dormancy period and absence of financial demands rule out organized crime; the strategic target and the resources needed to remain hidden for 14 months indicate a state-sponsored actor.
Pitfall — Shadow IT ≠ deliberate attack: Shadow IT is not a malicious actor in the classical sense: it is a risk introduced unknowingly by employees who use unapproved tools for convenience. The exam might present a scenario where a breach occurs through an uncatalogued SaaS app used by a marketing manager. The correct answer is not "malicious insider threat" but "shadow IT" — the difference is in intentionality: absent in shadow IT, present in malicious insider threat.

Objective 2.2 — Threat vectors and attack surfaces

A threat vector is the path or mechanism an actor uses to reach their objective — the "how" they enter the system. The attack surface is the set of all exposed points through which an actor could potentially attack: every exposed service, every installed software, every user with access to a system contributes to expanding it. Reducing the attack surface is one of the fundamental principles of hardening. Objective 2.2 lists the main vectors that appear in the exam.

Message-based vectors

Message-based vectors exploit communication channels to deliver malicious payloads or trick users into performing harmful actions. They are the most frequent attack vectors by far because anyone with an email address, phone number, or instant messaging account is a potential target.

Email
The number one attack vector in breach statistics. A malicious email can contain: attachments with macros or embedded exploits (Office documents, PDF), links to phishing sites, or payloads directly in the message body. Email is effective because it easily passes the network perimeter (port 25/SMTP is often open) and leverages the user's trust toward known or apparently legitimate senders. Protection techniques: anti-spam filters, attachment sandboxing, DMARC/DKIM/SPF, user training.
SMS (Short Message Service)
Text messages are a growing vector for smishing attacks (phishing via SMS). The user receives an SMS simulating a bank notification, a parcel in delivery, or a compromised account warning, with a link to a malicious page. SMS has much higher open rates than email and users often lower their guard on personal mobile devices.
Instant Messaging (IM)
Platforms such as Teams, Slack, WhatsApp, Telegram, or Discord are used to send malicious links, infected files, or to engage in social engineering conversations. Often corporate security policies focus on email while neglecting IM channels, which therefore become a less controlled path. Spear phishing via IM is particularly effective in corporate contexts.
Exam tip — Message-based vectors: When the exam describes an attack received on a mobile device via a text message with a link → smishing via SMS. If the attack arrives on Teams or Slack in a work context → IM vector. The distinction between the three sub-vectors (email / SMS / IM) is often what differentiates answer options.

Image-based, file-based, and voice-call vectors

Image-based
An apparently innocent image file can hide malicious code through techniques such as steganography (data hidden in the least significant bits of pixels) or exploit vulnerabilities in image parsers (e.g., buffer overflow in the rendering of a malformed PNG or JPEG). Image-based attacks are also used to exfiltrate data by hiding information in images that are transmitted externally. Less common but particularly insidious because they are often not checked by traditional filters.
File-based
Any type of file can potentially be a vector: Office documents with malicious macros, PDFs with JavaScript exploits, ZIP archives that exploit zip-slip vulnerabilities (path traversal), trojaned executable installers, or scripts (.bat, .ps1, .vbs) with hidden payloads. The file-based vector is often combined with others (e.g., email attachment, file on USB, download from compromised site).
Voice call
Attacks conducted through voice calls. Vishing (voice phishing) is the most common technique: the attacker impersonates technical support, a bank, a government authority, or a colleague to extort credentials, OTP codes, or make the user perform actions (install remote access software, make wire transfers). Artificial intelligence is making voice calls even more dangerous through voice cloning: it is possible to replicate the voice of a company executive with just a few seconds of sample audio.
Practical example — vishing with AI voice cloning: A CFO receives a call from the voice — perfectly recognizable — of the CEO asking to make an urgent wire transfer to a foreign account before market close. This technique is called deepfake audio and has already been documented in real attacks that have caused losses of millions of dollars. Countermeasure: always verify the request through a second independent communication channel before executing urgent financial transactions.

Removable devices

Removable device
USB drives, external hard disks, SD cards, CD/DVD, and any physical media that can be connected to a system to transfer data or code. Removable devices completely bypass network defenses: an infected USB inserted into a corporate laptop brings malware directly onto the system without ever passing through a firewall or IPS. This technique was used in the Stuxnet attack (2010) against air-gapped Iranian nuclear facilities. Countermeasures include disabling USB ports via group policy, endpoint control (EDR), and use of DLP systems that detect insertion of unauthorized devices.
Pitfall — Air-gap ≠ absolute security: An air-gapped system (completely isolated from the network) is not immune to attacks if physical devices can be introduced into the environment. The removable device vector is specifically designed to overcome physical isolation. The exam might ask which vector is most effective against air-gapped systems: the answer is removable device, not network-based.

Vulnerable software

Vulnerable software — Client-based
Software installed locally on the user's device (browser, email client, PDF readers, Office suite, plugins) that contains known or zero-day vulnerabilities. An attacker can exploit these vulnerabilities through a malicious file, a specially crafted web page (drive-by download), or a link that triggers the exploit when the file is opened by the client application. Requires that the software be installed and run by the end user.
Vulnerable software — Agentless
Unlike client-based, agentless vectors do not require software installed on the victim's device: they exploit protocols, services, or web interfaces directly accessible via the network (e.g., a login page with SQL injection, an unauthenticated API endpoint, an exposed RPC service). They are often more scalable because they do not depend on user interaction and can be probed automatically by vulnerability scanners.

Characteristic Client-based Agentless

Unsupported systems and applications

Unsupported systems and applications
An operating system or application for which the manufacturer has ended support and no longer releases security patches (End-of-Life — EoL). Windows XP, Windows 7, legacy versions of Java, PHP 5.x are classic examples. These systems accumulate known and unpatched vulnerabilities over time, becoming preferred targets because exploits are publicly documented (CVE) and functional. They are often found in OT/ICS environments, hospitals, ATMs, and embedded systems where updating is complex or expensive. Mitigation when updating is not possible includes network segmentation, compensating controls, and intensive monitoring.
Exam tip — EoL vs. unsupported: The exam uses the terms interchangeably. When a scenario describes a system that cannot be updated or a software for which the vendor no longer releases patches, the vector category is "unsupported systems/applications". The preferred mitigation answer is network segmentation + compensating controls, not simple risk acceptance.

Unsecure networks

Wireless (Unsecure Wi-Fi networks)
Public or corporate Wi-Fi networks configured insecurely (without WPA3/WPA2 encryption, with obsolete WEP, without 802.1X authentication, or with SSIDs impersonating legitimate networks). An attacker in physical proximity can conduct evil twin attacks (rogue AP that replicates the name of the legitimate network), capture unencrypted traffic, or conduct MITM (man-in-the-middle) attacks on HTTP sessions. Employees who connect to public Wi-Fi from corporate laptops potentially expose corporate credentials.
Wired (Unsecure wired networks)
Wired networks lacking segmentation, without 802.1X authentication on switch ports, with uncontrolled physical access to network jacks. An attacker with physical access to an unattended area (conference room, hallway, reception) can connect a laptop or rogue device to the internal network. VLAN hopping (jumping between VLANs via double tagging) is also an attack specific to poorly configured wired networks.
Bluetooth
Bluetooth introduces a short-range but often overlooked wireless attack surface. Main vulnerabilities: Bluejacking (unsolicited sending of messages), Bluesnarfing (unauthorized access to device data), Bluebugging (device control). Devices that leave Bluetooth always on in discoverable mode amplify the risk. Bluetooth headsets, keyboards, and wireless mice in corporate environments are an often underestimated vector.

Open service ports and default credentials

Open service ports
Every network service listening on a TCP/UDP port is a potential attack vector. Unnecessary open ports expand the attack surface by exposing services that may be vulnerable. An Nmap scanner that finds port 3389 (RDP) open on the internet knows it can try brute force or exploit known RDP vulnerabilities (e.g., BlueKeep). The least functionality principle requires closing all non-strictly-necessary ports and limiting access to necessary ports only to authorized IPs.
Default credentials
Routers, switches, firewalls, printers, IP cameras, SCADA systems, and IoT devices are shipped with publicly documented default credentials (admin/admin, admin/password, root/root). If these credentials are not changed at installation, anyone who knows the device model — information often retrievable from banners or HTTP headers — can access it. Shodan.io is a search engine that indexes internet-connected devices with default credentials, making this vector trivially exploitable on a global scale.
Practical example — default credentials on IP cameras: A penetration tester performs a Shodan scan for the brand of IP cameras installed on a university campus. Finds 14 cameras reachable on the internet with a web interface on port 80. Tries the default credentials (admin/1234): 12 out of 14 grant immediate access. They now have access to the live feed of classrooms, corridors, and laboratories — and potentially to the internal network to which the cameras are connected. Cost of the attack: 0. Cost of prevention: 5 minutes per device at the time of installation.
Pitfall — Open ports ≠ confirmed vulnerability: An open port indicates a listening service, not necessarily a vulnerability. The vulnerability depends on the software version, configuration, and existence of available exploits. The exam may present scenarios where a port scan is the first step of an attack (reconnaissance) but not the attack itself.

Supply chain

The supply chain vector exploits the trust that an organization places in its suppliers, vendors, and service providers. Instead of attacking the target organization directly — often well-protected — the attacker compromises a link in the supply chain with lesser security posture, using that access point as a springboard. Supply chain attacks are particularly dangerous because the compromised code or devices arrive with the signature and trust of a known supplier.

Managed Service Providers (MSP)
Companies that manage IT, security, or infrastructure on behalf of their clients have privileged access to the systems of many organizations simultaneously. Compromising an MSP potentially means having access to tens or hundreds of clients in a single operation. The Kaseya VSA attack (2021) is the most cited example: the criminals exploited the remote management platform of an MSP to distribute ransomware to approximately 1,500 client organizations simultaneously.
Vendors (Software vendors)
Compromising the build process or repository of a software vendor allows inserting malicious code into signed software updates distributed automatically to customers. The SolarWinds attack (2020) is the iconic example: the attackers inserted a backdoor (SUNBURST) into the legitimate update package of SolarWinds Orion, which was then downloaded and installed by approximately 18,000 organizations, including US government agencies.
Suppliers (Hardware suppliers)
The compromise can also occur at the hardware level: chips with backdoors inserted during manufacturing, firmware altered before delivery, counterfeit components with malicious functionality. The risk is particularly high for components produced in countries with less transparency about production processes. Countermeasures include firmware integrity verification, procurement from certified suppliers, and monitoring for anomalies in device behavior post-installation.

Type Point of compromise Historical example Impact scale

Exam tip — Supply chain attacks: When a scenario describes an organization compromised despite its own defenses being up to date, and the compromise arrives through legitimate software, an automatic update, or a third-party vendor, the vector is supply chain. The keyword is implicit trust toward a third party that is being exploited.

Human vectors / Social engineering

Social engineering exploits human psychology rather than technical vulnerabilities. It is the attack vector most difficult to counter because there is no patch for human behavior. It exploits principles of psychology such as authority, urgency, reciprocity, scarcity, and fear. Objective 2.2 includes a specific list of techniques that must be known for the exam. Chapter CH4 further explores the more complex techniques.

Phishing
A fraud technique in which the attacker sends communications — typically via email — that impersonate legitimate senders (banks, cloud services, well-known companies) to induce the victim to provide credentials, click malicious links, or open infected attachments. Spear phishing is the targeted variant aimed at a specific individual, with personalized messages that significantly increase credibility. Whaling is spear phishing directed at high-level executives.
Vishing (Voice phishing)
Phishing conducted via voice call. The attacker impersonates technical support, a banking institution, a tax authority, or a colleague to extort sensitive information or make the victim perform dangerous actions. See also the "Voice call" section above. The combination of vishing with AI voice cloning is a concrete emerging threat.
Smishing (SMS phishing)
Phishing via SMS. The attacker sends a text message with a malicious link, often disguised as a delivery notification, bank account verification, or security alert. Click-through rates on SMS links are historically higher than via email because users are less trained to recognize warning signs on personal mobile devices.
Misinformation / Disinformation
Misinformation is the unintentional spread of false information; disinformation is the intentional spread of false information to manipulate opinions or behaviors. In the context of information security, disinformation campaigns can be used by nation-states to influence elections, destabilize organizations, discredit competitors, or create confusion during a security incident (e.g., making people believe a system is secure when it is compromised). They are also used to poison search results that a company conducts about itself.
Impersonation
The attacker poses as another person — a colleague, an IT technician, a bank official, an inspector — to gain physical access or confidential information. Unlike phishing (digital), impersonation can occur in person or by phone. Classic technique: an individual presents themselves at the office entrance pretending to be the photocopier technician to gain unaccompanied access to the premises.
Business Email Compromise (BEC)
A highly targeted attack in which the attacker compromises or impersonates a corporate email account — often the CEO or CFO — to authorize fraudulent wire transfers or request sensitive data. BEC causes some of the highest financial losses in cybercrime (billions of dollars annually according to the FBI). The key of the attack is credibility: the email appears to come from an authorized sender inside the organization. Countermeasures: phone verification policy for urgent wire transfers, MFA on email accounts, DMARC.
Pretexting
Creation of an invented but convincing scenario (pretext) to gain the victim's trust and induce them to provide information or perform actions. The attacker builds a fictitious identity with a credible story: "I am from the central IT division and I need to verify your credentials for a security audit". Pretexting is the narrative component that makes any other social engineering technique credible.
Watering hole
The attacker compromises a website regularly visited by the targets (industry forum, supplier site, professional community portal) and infects it with malicious code. Anyone who visits the compromised site with a vulnerable browser is automatically infected (drive-by download). The name derives from the analogy with predators waiting for prey at watering holes in the savanna. It is a particularly effective attack against employees of specific sectors (defense, energy, finance) who frequent the same industry sites.
Brand impersonation
The attacker creates emails, websites, or social profiles that faithfully imitate a well-known brand (Microsoft, Apple, Amazon, PayPal, DHL) to deceive the user. Logos, layout, and tone are copied almost perfectly. The typical objective is to collect access credentials or payment data. Differs from generic phishing by the specificity of the imitated brand and the quality of the imitation.
Typosquatting
Registration of domains with typographical errors compared to known legitimate sites: paypa1.com, microsoftt.com, goggle.com, arnazon.com. Anyone who types the wrong URL by mistake lands on a site controlled by the attacker. Variant: combo squatting adds plausible words to the legitimate domain (e.g., microsoft-support.com). Typosquatting is often used to distribute malware, collect credentials, or display fraudulent advertising.

Exam tip — Social engineering scenarios: The SY0-701 exam often presents scenarios where you must identify the correct social engineering technique. Discriminating criteria are: channel (email → phishing; SMS → smishing; phone → vishing), who is being impersonated (CEO/CFO + wire transfer request → BEC), compromised website frequented by targets → watering hole, similar URL with errors → typosquatting. Memorize these channel-technique pairings because the options in questions are often very similar to each other.
Pitfall — Phishing vs. Spear phishing vs. Whaling: Generic phishing = indiscriminate mass campaign. Spear phishing = targeted campaign on a specific individual or group, with customized content (name, role, real references). Whaling = spear phishing specifically aimed at high-level executives (whale = "big fish"). The exam uses these terms precisely: if the message contains specific details about the victim, it is spear phishing, not generic phishing.

Summary flashcards — CH2

  • Nation-state — State-sponsored actor, unlimited resources, high sophistication, strategic objectives (espionage, war, disruption of critical infrastructure).
  • Insider threat — Internal threat: a subject with legitimate access who acts harmfully (malicious) or causes unintentional damage (negligent).
  • Hacktivist — Attacker driven by political/ideological beliefs; objective is reputational damage or public visibility, not financial gain.
  • Shadow IT — Unapproved systems/services used by employees; expand the attack surface outside official IT controls.
  • APT (Advanced Persistent Threat) — Nation-state attack characterized by high sophistication, long persistence (months/years), and specific strategic objectives.
  • Threat vector — The path/mechanism used by the attacker to reach the target (email, USB, network, supply chain...).
  • Attack surface — The set of all potentially exploitable exposed points; reduced by closing ports, disabling unnecessary services, and updating software.
  • Smishing — Phishing via SMS; the user receives a text message with a malicious link or request for sensitive data.
  • Vishing — Phishing via voice call; the attacker impersonates a bank, technical support, or authority to extort credentials or OTP.
  • BEC (Business Email Compromise) — Compromise or impersonation of a corporate email account to authorize fraudulent wire transfers; causes billion-dollar financial losses.
  • Watering hole — The attacker compromises a site frequented by targets and infects visitors via drive-by download.
  • Typosquatting — Registration of domains with typographical errors (paypa1.com) to capture users who type the wrong URL.
  • Supply chain attack — Compromise of a supplier/vendor/MSP to then reach client organizations; exploits implicit trust toward third parties (e.g., SolarWinds, Kaseya).
  • Default credentials — Factory credentials not changed; one of the most trivial and widespread vectors, particularly on routers, IP cameras, and IoT.
  • Removable device — USB/external hard disks used to bring malware into air-gapped systems or exfiltrate data bypassing network controls (e.g., Stuxnet).
  • Pretexting — Creation of a credible narrative scenario to gain the victim's trust; it is the "storytelling" component common to many social engineering attacks.
  • Client-based vs. Agentless — Client-based: requires software installed on the victim device (browser, plugin); Agentless: exploits network services/protocols without client-side installed software.
  • Unsupported systems (EoL) — Systems with no more security patches; mitigation when not updatable: network segmentation + compensating controls.
