Objective 2.2 — Human Vectors / Social Engineering

Social engineering is the discipline of attacks against the human factor. Instead of exploiting a technical vulnerability, the attacker manipulates people into taking actions against their own interest: revealing credentials, opening attachments, authorizing payments, or allowing physical access. Objective 2.2 of SY0-701 explicitly lists techniques that the candidate must recognize, distinguish, and connect to realistic scenarios. Understanding the psychological mechanisms behind these attacks is essential because exam questions often present narrative situations where similar answer choices must be separated precisely.

Psychological principles of social engineering

Social engineering attacks do not work by accident. They exploit cognitive mechanisms that push people to act quickly instead of stopping to verify. Knowing these principles helps identify an attack in progress and design more effective user awareness training.

Authority
The victim complies because the request appears to come from a figure of power: the CEO, the IT help desk, law enforcement, or an auditor. In business environments this lever is extremely effective: an email apparently signed by the CEO requesting an urgent wire transfer may be executed without verification by someone afraid of the consequences of refusal.
Intimidation
The attacker uses explicit or implicit threats — termination, legal consequences, account suspension, fines — to create fear and suppress critical thinking. It is often combined with authority, for example: “I am from compliance; if you do not provide the credentials immediately, I will open a disciplinary case.”
Consensus / Social Proof
The victim is persuaded because they are told, or led to believe, that other colleagues or users have already done the same thing. This principle exploits the human tendency to use other people’s behavior as guidance in uncertain situations.
Scarcity / Urgency
Something appears more valuable or necessary when it is perceived as limited in time or availability. Urgency removes the time needed to think, verify, or ask a colleague for confirmation. “You have 24 hours to verify your account before suspension” is a typical example.
Familiarity / Liking
People trust individuals they know or like more easily. An attacker may build this trust over time through preliminary interactions or imitate the communication style, references, and tone of known senders. Even using the victim’s first name can increase perceived familiarity.
Trust
The attacker presents themselves as a trusted entity — a long-standing supplier, a certificate authority, a bank, or a well-known brand — and abuses the victim’s existing trust. Brand impersonation and BEC rely on institutional trust rather than personal relationships.
Exam Tip — Objective 2.2: Social engineering questions often ask which psychological principle is being exploited. Read the action verb in the story: if the victim obeys, think authority; if they rush because of consequences, think intimidation plus urgency; if they trust the request because “everyone else did it,” think consensus.

Phishing and variants (Objective 2.2)

Phishing is the family of attacks that uses fraudulent communication to trick victims into providing credentials, clicking malicious links, or opening infected attachments. SY0-701 explicitly distinguishes variants by channel and targeting, and the exam often uses similar terms as distractors.

Phishing
A large-scale attack usually delivered by email. Messages imitate legitimate senders such as banks, cloud services, or couriers. The goal is to maximize the number of victims with a single broad campaign. Common signs include look-alike domains, urgent wording, links pointing to different URLs than the displayed text, and unexpected attachments.
Spear Phishing
A targeted variant aimed at a specific person or small group. The attacker personalizes the message using real information about the victim, such as name, job role, manager, or current project, often gathered through OSINT. This personalization makes the message much more credible.
Whaling
Spear phishing directed specifically at executives, C-level leaders, or senior managers. The value of the target justifies greater preparation. Typical themes include legal documents, merger and acquisition material, financial approvals, and confidential board communications.
Vishing (Voice Phishing)
Phishing conducted by voice call. The attacker may impersonate the help desk, a bank, a vendor, or a public authority. Modern vishing is made more dangerous by caller ID spoofing and AI voice cloning, which can make the caller sound like a trusted person.
Smishing (SMS Phishing)
Phishing delivered through SMS or mobile messaging. It often uses short links, fake parcel delivery notifications, fake bank alerts, or urgent account-verification messages. Mobile interfaces make URL inspection harder, which increases click-through rates.
Exam Tip — Phishing variants: The channel and the target decide the answer. Generic email campaign: phishing. Personalized email: spear phishing. Executive target: whaling. Phone call: vishing. SMS: smishing.

Misinformation and Disinformation (Objective 2.2)

Both terms refer to false or misleading information, but the intent is different. The exam tests this difference directly: misinformation is false information spread without deliberate malicious intent, while disinformation is intentionally created or distributed to deceive, manipulate, or cause harm.

Misinformation
False or inaccurate information shared unintentionally. A user may forward a fake security warning because they believe it is true. The damage can still be significant, but the key point is the absence of deliberate deception.
Disinformation
False information deliberately produced or distributed to manipulate perception, influence behavior, damage reputation, or support an adversary’s goal. In cybersecurity contexts it can be used to spread false breach claims, create panic, or redirect defenders away from the real attack.
Pitfall — Intent matters: Do not classify based only on whether the information is false. Classify based on whether the falsehood was spread intentionally.

Impersonation and Pretexting (Objective 2.2)

Impersonation and pretexting often appear together. Impersonation is the act of pretending to be someone or something else. Pretexting is the invented scenario that makes the request believable. In practical attacks, the pretext gives the impersonation context and urgency.

Impersonation
The attacker pretends to be a trusted person, team, vendor, authority, or service. The goal is to borrow the credibility of the impersonated identity and obtain information, access, money, or action from the victim.
Pretexting
The attacker builds a plausible story to justify the request. For example, “I am from the help desk and we are migrating your mailbox today” is a pretext that supports a credential-harvesting attempt. Strong pretexts include names, dates, project references, ticket numbers, or business context.
Practical example — Help desk pretext: An attacker calls an employee claiming to be from IT and says a mailbox migration is failing. The victim is asked to approve an MFA prompt or read a one-time code. The attacker is impersonating IT; the migration story is the pretext.

Business Email Compromise — BEC (Objective 2.2)

Business Email Compromise is a social engineering attack that uses a compromised, spoofed, or convincingly imitated business email account to trigger financial fraud or unauthorized business action. BEC is especially dangerous because it often contains no malware and may bypass purely technical filters.

Business Email Compromise (BEC)
The attacker impersonates an executive, supplier, attorney, finance employee, or trusted business partner to request a wire transfer, change payment details, buy gift cards, send tax data, or disclose sensitive documents. The strongest mitigations are MFA, payment verification workflows, separation of duties, and out-of-band confirmation using a trusted channel.
Exam Tip — BEC: When the scenario involves a finance employee receiving a convincing business email requesting a payment change or urgent transfer, the answer is BEC. The best response is not “train users” alone, but a verified business process such as callback validation and approval separation.

Watering Hole (Objective 2.2)

A watering hole attack compromises a website that the intended victims already visit regularly. Instead of sending the malicious content directly to the victims, the attacker infects a trusted place and waits for the victims to arrive.

Watering Hole Attack
The attacker identifies websites commonly used by a target group — industry portals, supplier sites, professional associations, or internal-facing partner portals — and compromises one of them. Visitors may receive malicious JavaScript, a drive-by download, or a credential-harvesting page. This technique can bypass email defenses because the victim initiates the visit to a familiar site.
Practical example — Sector portal: A defense contractor’s employees regularly visit a specialized supplier portal. An attacker compromises that portal and injects malicious code that profiles visitors and delivers an exploit only to users from the contractor’s IP range.

Brand Impersonation and Typosquatting (Objective 2.2)

These techniques abuse trust in names and domains. Brand impersonation imitates the visual identity and communication style of a legitimate organization, while typosquatting registers domains that exploit typing errors or visual similarity.

Brand Impersonation
The attacker imitates a known brand’s logo, colors, wording, email templates, or login page. The victim trusts the message because it appears to come from a familiar organization. This is common in credential harvesting and fake support scams.
Typosquatting (URL Hijacking)
The attacker registers domains similar to legitimate ones, relying on spelling mistakes, missing letters, swapped characters, or look-alike characters. Examples include amazn.example instead of amazon.example, or domains that replace letters with visually similar characters. Typosquatting differs from pharming, which manipulates DNS resolution rather than user typing.

Objective 2.4 — Password Attacks

Password attacks attempt to obtain or guess authentication secrets. The exam focuses on recognizing the difference between brute force and password spraying, because the two attacks have different patterns, indicators, and mitigations. Brute force concentrates many guesses on one account or hash. Password spraying spreads a small number of common passwords across many accounts to avoid lockout.

Brute Force (Objective 2.4)

Brute Force Attack
An attacker tries many password candidates against a single account, service, or captured hash until the correct password is found. Online brute force is limited by lockout policies, throttling, and MFA. Offline brute force against stolen hashes can be much faster because the attacker is no longer constrained by the authentication server.
Practical example — Online brute force: A VPN account receives hundreds of failed login attempts in a few minutes from the same source. The account locks out repeatedly. This is a classic brute-force pattern.
Exam Tip — Brute force: Many passwords against one account usually indicates brute force. The visible indicator is often repeated failed authentication and account lockout.

Password Spraying (Objective 2.4)

Password Spraying
An attacker tries one or a few common passwords against many accounts. The goal is to avoid per-account lockout thresholds. Examples include trying Spring2026! or CompanyName2026! against every user in the directory. Detection requires correlation across many users because each individual account may show only one or two failures.
Practical example — Distributed spraying: A SIEM shows one failed login for 600 different users within 20 minutes, all using the same password pattern. No single account locks out, but the organization-wide pattern reveals password spraying.
Exam Tip — Password spraying: One or a few passwords against many accounts indicates spraying. Look for distributed failures across the user base rather than repeated failures on a single account.

Objective 2.4 — Indicators of Malicious Activity related to passwords and social engineering

Indicators of malicious activity are observable signs that suggest a compromise, attempted attack, or active abuse. No single indicator always proves an incident, but correlation across multiple indicators provides high-confidence evidence. In password and social engineering scenarios, authentication patterns, geolocation anomalies, missing logs, and sudden resource changes are especially important.

Indicators one by one

Account Lockout
Repeated failed login attempts cause the account to lock. This may indicate brute force, password spraying, mistyped credentials, or a misconfigured service account. It becomes more suspicious when correlated with source IPs, time windows, and multiple affected accounts.
Concurrent Session Usage
The same account is active from different locations, devices, or sessions at the same time. This can indicate credential compromise, session hijacking, or token theft, especially when the locations or devices do not match the user’s normal behavior.
Blocked Content
Security controls repeatedly block access to malicious URLs, command-and-control domains, prohibited attachments, or suspicious downloads. This may indicate phishing follow-up activity, malware trying to communicate externally, or attempted data exfiltration.
Impossible Travel
The same account authenticates from locations that are physically impossible to travel between in the observed time window. For example, a login from Rome followed ten minutes later by a login from Singapore strongly suggests credential compromise unless a VPN or proxy explains the pattern.
Resource Consumption
Unusual CPU, RAM, disk, network, or bandwidth consumption may indicate cryptomining, ransomware encryption, worm propagation, data staging, or automated scanning. Baseline monitoring is required to distinguish normal peaks from malicious behavior.
Resource Inaccessibility
Files, applications, shares, or services suddenly become unavailable or encrypted. This is a strong ransomware or denial-of-service indicator, especially when combined with unusual file extensions, ransom notes, or mass file modifications.
Out-of-Cycle Logging
Activity appears outside the expected schedule, such as administrative logins at 3 a.m., batch jobs running on the wrong day, or access attempts outside the user’s normal working hours. The key is comparison against a historical baseline.
Published / Documented
Information about the organization or its systems appears on underground forums, paste sites, dark web markets, or threat intelligence feeds. Examples include leaked credentials, internal IP ranges, or exploit discussions targeting a software version used internally.
Missing Logs
Gaps or deletions appear in system, application, or security logs. An attacker with privileged access may delete or alter logs to cover their tracks. This is why logs should be transmitted in real time to a centralized SIEM or immutable storage.
Exam Tip — Indicators: Match the anomaly to the correct category. Failed authentication: account lockout. Same user from two countries in minutes: impossible travel. Encrypted or unavailable files: resource inaccessibility. Gaps in logs: missing logs. Activity at unusual hours: out-of-cycle logging. Credentials on a paste site: published/documented.

  • Smishing — Phishing by SMS; mobile users often inspect links less carefully.
  • Misinformation vs Disinformation — Misinformation is false but not intentional; disinformation is deliberately false and manipulative.
  • Pretexting — The invented story that makes a request credible.
  • BEC — Compromised or imitated business email used for financial fraud; mitigate with MFA and out-of-band verification.
  • Watering Hole — Compromise of a site regularly visited by the intended victims.
  • Typosquatting — A domain exploiting user typing mistakes or visual similarity.
  • Brute Force — Many passwords against one account; often causes account lockout.

  • Impossible Travel — Same account authenticates from two locations too far apart in too little time.
