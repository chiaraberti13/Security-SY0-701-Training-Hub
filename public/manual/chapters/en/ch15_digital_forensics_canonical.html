Objective 4.8 (partial) — Digital Forensics

Digital forensics is the discipline that allows collecting, preserving, analyzing, and presenting digital evidence in a methodical, repeatable, and legally defensible manner. It is not simply about recovering files from a compromised system: every action must follow precise procedures so that evidence remains intact and admissible. A procedural error — such as booting the system before acquiring its memory — can render the entire investigation unusable in legal proceedings.

The field applies in different contexts: civil and criminal litigation, internal investigations of employees, cybersecurity incident response, and support for intelligence operations. In all these cases the common thread is the same: collect data without altering it, document every step, and be able to demonstrate the integrity of the process.

What is digital forensics

Digital forensics deals with digital artifacts: any element generated by the use of computer systems that can help reconstruct events. Artifacts include log files, RAM contents, disk images, captured network traffic, metadata, virtual machine snapshots, system registries, and much more.

A valid forensic activity must produce documented observations, conclusions supported by traceable evidence, coherent timelines built from timestamps and metadata, and documentation sufficient for the work to be verified or repeated by independent third parties. This last point — reproducibility — is what distinguishes forensic analysis from a simple technical examination.

Digital Artifact
Any digital element that can contribute to reconstructing events on a system: files, logs, memory contents, metadata, network traffic, snapshots, configuration registries. Not all artifacts have the same evidentiary value: relevance depends on the nature of the investigation and context.
Forensic Acquisition
The process of creating a faithful and verifiable copy of a medium or data source. It differs from a logical copy because it reproduces the entire physical content of the medium — including unallocated spaces, deleted files, and slack space — without modifying the original data.
Write Blocker
A hardware device or software function that allows reading a medium while preventing any writes. Its use is mandatory in forensic acquisition: without a write blocker, even simply mounting a disk can update timestamps or metadata, compromising the integrity of the evidence.
Forensic Hash
A cryptographic value (typically MD5 or SHA-256) calculated on the original image and on the acquired copy. If the two hashes match, the copy is bit-for-bit identical to the original. The hash is the primary mechanism by which the integrity of a digital exhibit is demonstrated.
EXAM TIP 4.8-A — In scenario questions on digital forensics, the correct sequence is always: do not touch anything → document the initial state → acquire with write blocker → calculate hash → verify integrity. If the question asks what is the first thing to do, the answer almost always concerns preservation or documentation, not analysis.
Practical example — Acquisition of a compromised laptop
An employee is suspected of data exfiltration. The laptop is seized. The analyst does not turn it on, connects the disk to a hardware write blocker, runs dd if=/dev/sda of=/evidence/laptop.img bs=4M, calculates the SHA-256 hash of the image and compares it with the hash of the original disk. Only if they match is the image accepted as forensic evidence. The entire procedure is documented on the chain of custody form.

Legal Hold and Preservation

When an organization is involved in actual or anticipated litigation, it may receive a legal hold notice (also called a litigation hold): a legal directive requiring the suspension of any ordinary processes that might alter, delete, or render irrecoverable data potentially relevant to the case. This notice takes effect immediately and applies to all types of media: email, files on servers, backups, mobile devices, system logs.

Preservation is the operational phase of the legal hold: it consists of implementing all technical and organizational measures necessary to ensure that data is not modified. This may include suspending automatic deletion policies, creating backup copies, disabling circular log overwriting, and isolating storage systems.

The greatest risk at this stage is spoliation of evidence: the alteration, destruction, concealment, or negligent handling of relevant evidence, even unintentionally. Courts can draw negative inferences from spoliation — for example, assuming that destroyed data was unfavorable to the party that lost it. This is why organizations must have legal hold procedures ready before receiving the notification.

Legal Hold (Litigation Hold)
A legal order or obligation requiring the complete preservation of all data potentially relevant to a proceeding. It suspends retention policies, automatic deletion, and overwriting. Those who receive it have an immediate obligation to apply it to all involved data custodians.
Preservation
The set of technical and organizational measures adopted to ensure that data subject to a legal hold is not modified, deleted, or rendered irrecoverable. Includes suspension of circular backups, read-only copying, isolation of storage systems, and notification to responsible employees.
Spoliation of Evidence
Destruction, alteration, concealment, delay in delivery, or improper withholding of relevant evidence. It can be intentional or negligent. Legal consequences include procedural sanctions, negative inference (the judge can presume that destroyed data was unfavorable), and in more serious cases, criminal charges.
PITFALL — Legal Hold vs Preservation
Legal hold is the legal notification that imposes the obligation of preservation — it is a legal act, issued by a lawyer or an authority. Preservation is the technical and operational response to that notification — it is what the IT department and CISO concretely do. The exam distinguishes them: if the question asks "who issues the legal hold?" the answer is the legal team; if it asks "how do you respond?" the answer concerns preservation measures.
EXAM TIP 4.8-B — If a scenario describes a company that continued to automatically delete emails after receiving a legal notification, the correct term is spoliation. This is not a technical error but a legal violation, even if unintentional. The organization is responsible from the moment it receives the legal hold.

E-discovery and EDRM

E-discovery (electronic discovery) is the process of identifying, collecting, reviewing, analyzing, and producing electronic information in response to a legal request. It is the digital version of traditional discovery: instead of searching for and producing paper documents, one works with files, emails, databases, logs, and any other form of electronic data.

The industry reference model is the EDRM (Electronic Discovery Reference Model), which organizes the process into sequential phases, each with precise objectives and responsibilities. Knowing the EDRM is important because it allows an organization to understand where it is in the process and what obligations it has in each phase.

EDRM Phase Objective Example activity
Information Governance Understand what data exists and how it is managed before a case arises Mapping storage systems, retention policies
Identification Identify where potentially relevant information is located Search on servers, email, cloud, mobile devices
Preservation Prevent modification or destruction of relevant data Legal hold, suspension of automatic deletion
Collection Collect data in a forensic and documented manner Image acquisition, email export, log copying
Processing Reduce volume, remove duplicates, prepare data De-duplication, text extraction, format conversion
Review Verify relevance and legal privileges of documents Review by lawyers, tagging, redaction
Analysis Identify patterns, subjects, timelines, and connections Metadata analysis, event timeline, keyword searches
Production Deliver documents to parties in agreed format Export to PDF, TIFF, native format with load file
Presentation Use evidence in court proceedings or negotiations Presentation at hearings, depositions, settlement
EXAM TIP 4.8-C — The exam does not require memorizing all EDRM phases in sequence, but it is important to understand that e-discovery is a structured process that goes from initial governance through to courtroom presentation. In scenario questions, if asked "which phase of e-discovery concerns blocking automatic deletion?" the answer is Preservation, not Collection.

Order of Volatility — Capture first what disappears first

The order of volatility is the fundamental principle that guides the acquisition sequence in a forensic investigation: the most volatile data — those that risk being irreversibly lost in the shortest time — are collected first. Shutting down a system without first acquiring RAM memory means permanently losing active processes, open network connections, loaded cryptographic keys, temporary credentials, and any data that had not yet been written to disk.

Volatility is not just a matter of shutting down: even trivial operations like opening a browser or running a command can overwrite the CPU cache, update the ARP table, or modify timestamps. This is why the analyst must intervene as little as possible on the live system, collecting data in the correct order.

Priority Data source Why it is volatile
1 — Highest CPU registers and L1/L2/L3 cache Changes with every clock cycle, lost at immediate shutdown
2 RAM (physical memory) Disappears at shutdown; contains processes, keys, connections
3 Swap space / pagefile Virtual extension of RAM on disk; partially persistent
4 Open network connections, ARP cache, routing table Cleared at disconnection or shutdown
5 Running processes, open handles Terminated at shutdown; not tracked on disk
6 Temporary files, kernel circular logs Overwritten periodically or at reboot
7 Hard disk / SSD (persistent data) Survives shutdown; can be modified by subsequent writes
8 — Lowest Remote backups, logs on centralized servers More persistent; require access to external systems
Practical example — Malware in memory
An analyst responds to an alert on a Windows server suspected of being compromised by fileless malware. Fileless malware lives exclusively in RAM and never writes to disk. If the analyst shuts down the server before acquiring memory, the malware — and all its traces — disappears forever. The correct procedure is: acquire RAM with FTK Imager or WinPmem while the system is still running, then acquire the disk, then shut it down.

Acquisition — Types of forensic acquisition

Forensic acquisition is the process of creating a verifiable copy of a data source without altering the original. There are different types of acquisition depending on the nature of the medium and the objective of the investigation.

Bit-by-Bit Copy (Physical image)
A sector-by-sector copy of the entire medium, including unallocated spaces, deleted files, slack space, and residues of previous data. It is the forensic gold standard because it preserves all physical content of the disk. Produced with tools like dd, FTK Imager, or dcfldd. Requires a write blocker to avoid altering the original.
Logical Copy
A copy of only the logical content: visible files and folders. Does not capture unallocated space, deleted files, or slack space. Faster and less space-consuming than a physical copy, but not suitable for investigations that require recovery of deleted data. Useful for e-discovery where only accessible content is of interest.
Memory Acquisition (RAM Dump)
Acquisition of the contents of the system's volatile memory. Essential for analyzing fileless malware, suspicious processes, active connections, and cryptographic keys. Common tools: FTK Imager (Windows), WinPmem, LiME (Linux Loadable Kernel Module).
Network Acquisition
Capture of network traffic via packet capture (PCAP). Requires access to a SPAN port (port mirror) on the switch or a network TAP. Tools: Wireshark, tcpdump, NetworkMiner. Allows analyzing communications, protocols used, and data transferred during the incident.
Cloud Acquisition
Acquisition of data from cloud environments (IaaS, SaaS, PaaS). Significantly more complex because it depends on provider cooperation, contracts (audit clause), jurisdiction, and availability of forensic APIs. Often limited to access logs, configurations, and snapshots rather than physical server content.
Virtual Machine / Container Acquisition
Acquisition of VM snapshots or data from containers. Snapshots preserve the complete state of the virtual machine at a precise moment. Containers are more difficult because they are ephemeral by nature: when terminated, they lose all data not persisted on external volumes.
PITFALL — Forensic copy vs logical copy
A logical copy made with normal backup software is not forensic evidence. Unallocated spaces, deleted files, and hash integrity verification are missing. If the exam question asks which type of copy is admissible as evidence, the answer is always the bit-by-bit copy with verified hash, not a traditional backup.
EXAM TIP 4.8-D — In cloud forensics scenarios, remember that the provider controls the physical infrastructure. An organization cannot simply "pull the disk" from a cloud server. It depends on the provider's APIs, the audit clause in the contract, and jurisdiction. If the question mentions difficulties in forensic acquisition in a cloud environment, the main obstacle is always the provider's control over physical hardware.

Chain of Custody

The chain of custody is the continuous and verifiable documentation of who has had access to a digital piece of evidence, when, for what purpose, and with what results. Every time an exhibit is transferred, examined, copied, or archived, the transfer is recorded with signature, date, time, and reason.

The purpose is to guarantee the admissibility of the evidence in legal proceedings. A defense attorney can challenge the entire evidentiary value of digital evidence if they demonstrate that the chain of custody had breaks or gaps — for example, if there is no documentation of who had access to the exhibit during a certain period. The chain of custody does not only protect against accusations of tampering: it also protects against unintentional human errors.

Chain of Custody
A documentary record that traces each custody transfer of an exhibit: who collected it, where it was stored, who examined it, when it was transferred, and what condition it was in at each transfer. Must be continuous from the moment of collection through presentation in court.
Admissibility
The requirement that evidence must meet to be accepted in legal proceedings. For digital evidence, the main criteria are: verifiable integrity via hash, documented chain of custody, acquisition with accepted methods, and absence of contamination. Inadmissible evidence cannot be used in court.
Provenance
Traceability of the origin of data: where it comes from, how it was acquired, what transformations it has undergone. Provenance is what allows an independent expert to retrace the process and verify its correctness.
Non-Repudiation
A property that makes it impossible to deny the authenticity of an action or data. In a forensic context, it is achieved through cryptographic hashes, digitally signed timestamps, and immutable logs. If a hash was calculated at acquisition and matches the one calculated in court, no one can claim that the evidence was altered.
Element to document Why it is critical
Unique identification of the exhibit Avoids confusion between multiple pieces of evidence; ensures traceability
Date and time of acquisition Temporal anchor for subsequent analyses
Identity of who collected the evidence Personal accountability in case of dispute
Hash of original and copy Integrity verification; basis of admissibility
Every custody transfer No temporal gaps; no opportunity for undocumented tampering
Storage conditions Evidence that the exhibit was not exposed to altering agents
EXAM TIP 4.8-E — In scenarios where someone has physical access to an exhibit without documentation, the correct answer always describes a violation of the chain of custody. The exam tests the understanding that even an unintentional undocumented access can make evidence contestable.

Reporting

The forensic report is the final product of the investigation: the document that translates technical work into understandable, evidence-supported conclusions, defensible in any context — legal, managerial, or technical. An excellent forensic analysis has zero value if it is not communicated effectively.

A good forensic report is complete but not redundant: it includes everything necessary to understand the process and conclusions, without overwhelming the reader with irrelevant details. It must be written so that even someone without deep technical expertise can understand what happened, while at the same time containing sufficient technical details for an independent expert to verify the work.

Executive Summary
The initial section of the report intended for management and legal counsel. Describes in accessible language what was investigated, what was found, and what the main conclusions are. Does not contain in-depth technical details.
Methodology Section
A section that describes the forensic process: tools used, tool versions, acquisition methods, configured parameters, assumptions made, and limitations encountered. Enables reproducibility of the process.
Findings Section
A section with detailed technical results for each examined medium or system. Includes artifacts found, event timeline, identified connections, and evidence supporting the conclusions.
Conclusions and Recommendations
A final summary that connects the evidence to conclusions and, when appropriate, includes operational recommendations to prevent the event from recurring.
Practical example — Ransomware response report
After a ransomware attack, the forensic report includes: (1) Executive summary for the board with estimated impact and timeline; (2) Methodology section explaining how images of encrypted servers were acquired; (3) Findings with the identified initial access vector (exposed RDP with weak credentials), lateral movement documented in Windows Event Logs, and the exact time of ransomware deployment; (4) Recommendations: disable public RDP, implement MFA, segment the network.

Objective 4.9 — Data sources to support an investigation

A security investigation depends on the ability to collect, correlate, and interpret data from heterogeneous sources. No single source is sufficient: reconstructing an incident requires correlation between network logs, system logs, endpoint data, detection tool alerts, and traffic captures. Objective 4.9 maps exactly these sources — what they produce, why they are useful, and when they are the right source to answer a specific investigative question.

Log Data

Logs are automatic records of events generated by systems, applications, and network devices. They are the most common data source in a security investigation. Their forensic value depends on three factors: timestamp accuracy (NTP synchronization), completeness (which events are logged and at what verbosity level), and integrity (logs have not been tampered with or deleted).

Firewall Logs

Firewall logs record traffic filtering decisions: permitted connections, blocked connections, applied rules, source and destination IP addresses, ports, protocols, and timestamps. They are fundamental for reconstructing suspicious communications, identifying port scanning attempts, detecting connections to malicious or C2 (command and control) IPs, and verifying whether an internal host attempted to communicate externally in an anomalous way.

The main limitation of firewall logs is that they only show traffic crossing the perimeter: lateral traffic (east-west) between internal systems is not recorded by a perimeter firewall. This is why modern architectures add internal firewalls and micro-segmentation.

EXAM TIP 4.9-A — If a scenario asks "where do you find evidence of an unauthorized connection to an external IP?" the primary answer is firewall log. If it asks "where do you find evidence of internal lateral movement?" perimeter firewall logs are not enough: internal network logs or endpoint logs are needed.

Application Logs

Application logs are generated by individual applications — web servers (Apache, Nginx, IIS), databases (MySQL, MSSQL), application servers, CRM, ERP — and record events specific to their operation: requests received, authentications, queries executed, errors, transactions, resource accesses. They are the primary source for investigating application-level compromises such as SQL injection, XSS, API abuse, or unauthorized access to features.

An Apache web server log records every HTTP request with method, URL, user agent, response code, and bytes transferred. In an investigation of a web intrusion, this log allows identifying the payload used in the attack, the exact time, and the resources accessed subsequently.

Practical example — SQL Injection in application logs
An Apache log contains: GET /search.php?q='%20OR%20'1'='1 HTTP/1.1 200. The URL-encoded string ' OR '1'='1 is a classic SQL injection signature. The 200 response code indicates that the request was accepted. The analyst correlates this event with the database log and finds an unusual query executed at the same timestamp, confirming the successful exploit.

Endpoint Logs

Endpoint logs come from workstations, laptops, and servers: they include process events (start/termination), filesystem accesses, registry changes, network connections initiated by the system, software installations, application errors. Tools like Sysmon (Windows) and auditd (Linux) greatly amplify endpoint visibility, recording events at very high granularity.

Endpoint logs are critical for detecting post-exploitation techniques: persistence (new services or registry keys), privilege escalation (token manipulation), credential dumping (LSASS access), and lateral movement (use of PsExec, WMI, SMB). Without endpoint logs, many of these techniques remain invisible.

OS-Specific Security Logs

OS-specific security logs are generated by audit mechanisms built into the operating system. On Windows, the primary one is the Windows Security Event Log (accessible via Event Viewer), which records events such as authentications (Event ID 4624/4625), account creation (4720), privilege escalation (4672), file access (4663), and policy changes (4719). On Linux, /var/log/auth.log and /var/log/secure record sudo authentications and SSH accesses.

These logs are often the first place to look for brute-forced credentials, accounts created by the attacker, access to sensitive files, and abuses of administrative privileges.

Windows Event ID Event Investigative relevance
4624 Successful logon Who authenticated, when and how (interactive, network, batch)
4625 Failed logon Failed attempts; brute force patterns
4648 Logon with explicit credentials Use of different credentials (pass-the-hash, RunAs)
4672 Special privileges assigned Assignment of elevated privileges to an account
4720 User account created Creation of backdoor accounts by the attacker
4688 New process created Launch of suspicious processes; fileless execution
EXAM TIP 4.9-B — In scenario questions asking where to find evidence of unauthorized access to a Windows system, the Windows Security Event Log is the expected answer. If the question specifies "remote access via stolen credentials," Event IDs 4624 (logon type = Network or RemoteInteractive) are the starting point.

IPS/IDS Logs

IDS/IPS logs (Intrusion Detection/Prevention System) contain alerts generated by matches between observed traffic and signatures of known attacks, or from anomalies detected by behavioral models. Each alert typically includes: timestamp, source and destination IP, triggered rule or signature, severity, and for IPS, whether traffic was blocked or just recorded.

The main value of IDS/IPS logs is pre-classification: the system has already identified the traffic as potentially malicious and correlated it to a specific attack technique. This reduces the analyst's work compared to manual analysis of raw packet capture. The limitation is the false positive rate, which requires triage capability.

PITFALL — IDS vs IPS in logs
An IDS generates alerts but does not block traffic: its logs show what was detected, but do not guarantee that the attack did not succeed. An IPS can block traffic: its logs show both alerts and blocking actions. In an investigation, an IDS alert without blocking means the attack could continue and other logs must be checked.

Network Logs

Network logs record the flow of network traffic at different levels of granularity. NetFlow / sFlow / IPFIX record metadata of network conversations (who talked to whom, when, how much traffic, which port) without preserving packet contents. DNS logs record all name resolution queries. DHCP logs track IP address assignments over time.

Network logs often contain evidence of lateral movement: if an internal host A suddenly starts making connections to dozens of other internal hosts on SMB ports, this pattern emerges clearly in NetFlow even without packet capture. Similarly, DNS queries to algorithmically generated domains (DGA) are visible in DNS logs before even analyzing the payload.

Metadata

Metadata is data about data: structured information associated with a file, email, image, or any other digital object, describing attributes such as author, creation date, modification date, size, tool used to create the file, geographical location (for photographs), and much more.

In a forensic investigation, metadata is often more revealing than the content itself. A Word document sent as proof of alibi might have in its metadata a last-modified timestamp that contradicts the narrative. A shared photograph might contain GPS coordinates in the EXIF metadata of where it was taken. Emails include routing metadata (headers) that reveal the real path of the message and the true IP of the sender.

File Metadata
Filesystem attributes associated with a file: creation timestamp (Created), last modification (Modified), last access (Accessed), and on NTFS, also when the file's metadata was modified (MFT Entry Modified). The acronym MACE (Modified, Accessed, Created, Entry Modified) is used in forensics to identify the four main timestamps.
Email Header Metadata
Routing information included in emails: origin server, IP of the client that sent the message, SMTP hops, timestamp of each hop, Message-ID. They allow tracing the true origin of an email even when the "From" field is forged.
EXIF Metadata
Metadata embedded in digital images: camera make and model, shooting settings, date and time, and — if the function is active — GPS coordinates of the location where the photo was taken. Often overlooked by users, they are a valuable source in investigations involving images.
EXAM TIP 4.9-C — In questions about metadata, remember that filesystem timestamps can be modified (timestomping is an anti-forensics technique used by attackers). The exam may present scenarios where file timestamps appear normal but other indicators contradict the timeline: in these cases, the correct answer will mention the risk of timestomping and the need to correlate multiple sources.

Data Sources

Beyond logs, a security investigation uses structured data sources produced by specialized tools: vulnerability scanners, SIEM platforms, operational dashboards, and traffic captures. These sources do not replace logs but complement them with pre-processed, aggregated, or contextualized information.

Vulnerability Scans

Vulnerability scan results produced by tools like Nessus, Qualys, OpenVAS, or Rapid7 InsightVM constitute a valuable data source in an investigation. Not only do they detect active vulnerabilities, but they document the security state of the system at a precise moment: installed software versions, open ports, active services, misconfigurations, and applicable CVEs.

In a post-incident investigation, comparing pre-incident scan results with post-incident scan results can reveal new installed services, ports opened by the attacker for persistence, and configuration changes. Additionally, vulnerability reports provide context on which exploit might have been used to compromise a vulnerable system.

Practical example — Vulnerability scan in post-breach investigation
An organization suffers a compromise. The forensic team runs a Nessus scan on the compromised server and discovers that it is listening on port 4444 (typical Metasploit reverse shell port) — a port that was not present in last week's scan. This is an immediate indicator that the attacker left an active backdoor, and the data emerges from the scanner results in seconds.

Automated Reports

Automated reports generated by SIEM, EDR (Endpoint Detection and Response), SOAR platforms, and compliance tools provide periodic summaries of security status: alert trends, top offenders, event summaries, configuration changes, compliance gaps. In an investigation, these reports provide historical context — they allow seeing whether an observed anomaly is new or part of a recurring pattern.

An automated SIEM report showing an anomalous spike in failed authentications the night before the incident can be evidence that the attacker conducted a brute force before gaining access — information that would be difficult to uncover by analyzing individual event logs one by one.

Dashboards

Security dashboards — in SIEMs (Splunk, QRadar, Microsoft Sentinel), EDR platforms (CrowdStrike Falcon, SentinelOne), or network monitoring tools (Darktrace, ExtraHop) — offer an aggregated, real-time view of security status. They allow an analyst to quickly identify visual anomalies that in raw logs would require hours of analysis.

In an investigative context, dashboards allow formulating initial hypotheses about the scope of an incident (how many systems are involved? what is the time window?) before diving into log details. They are particularly useful for initial triage and identifying which data sources to explore further.

EXAM TIP 4.9-D — Dashboards provide aggregated, real-time visibility but are not direct forensic evidence. In scenario questions asking which source to use to prove that an event occurred, the answer is logs or packet captures, not dashboards. Dashboards serve to detect and triage, not to document evidence.

Packet Captures

Network traffic captures (PCAP — Packet CAPture) are the most granular data source available in a network investigation: they record the complete content of every packet, including headers and payload. Tools like Wireshark, tcpdump, and NetworkMiner allow analyzing PCAPs to reconstruct TCP sessions, identify protocols used, extract transferred files, decode commands, and identify malware signatures in traffic.

Packet captures have two main limitations: size (traffic on high-speed networks generates enormous PCAPs in a short time) and encryption (HTTPS, TLS, VPN encrypt the payload, making only connection metadata visible). This is why modern organizations combine PCAPs with NetFlow analysis (for volume and metadata) and TLS inspection (decryption and re-encryption where allowed by policy).

PCAP (Packet CAPture)
A file containing the complete dump of network packets captured from an interface. It is the standard format for traffic capture, readable by Wireshark and most network analysis tools. A PCAP is considered forensic network evidence when acquired with appropriate integrity controls.
SPAN Port (Switch Port Analyzer)
A managed switch feature that replicates traffic from one or more ports (or an entire VLAN) to a monitoring port. It is the primary method for acquiring network traffic in a switched infrastructure without breaking existing connections.
Network TAP (Test Access Point)
An inline hardware device that physically copies network traffic to a monitoring system. Unlike a SPAN port, it does not consume switch resources and is considered more reliable for forensic acquisitions, because it cannot drop packets due to switch bandwidth limitations.

PITFALL — Packet capture vs NetFlow
The exam often contrasts these two sources. Packet capture preserves the complete content of every packet (header + payload) but produces enormous files and does not scale on fast networks. NetFlow preserves only the conversation metadata (who talked to whom, when, how much) without payload. If the question asks "which source allows reconstructing the exact content of an HTTP session?" the answer is packet capture. If it asks "which source scales on a high-traffic network to detect lateral movement?" the answer is NetFlow.
EXAM TIP 4.9-E — In scenario questions describing an investigation, the keyword "correlate" is the signal that multiple data sources must be used together. A SIEM is often the correct answer when the question asks how to correlate logs from different sources in a single point of analysis. Remember: the SIEM does not generate data, it aggregates and correlates from all other sources.

Summary flashcard

  • Digital Forensics — Discipline that acquires, preserves, analyzes, and documents digital evidence in a repeatable and legally defensible manner.
  • Legal Hold (Litigation Hold) — Legal order requiring complete preservation of all data relevant to a proceeding; suspends automatic deletion policies.
  • Preservation — Technical and operational response to a legal hold: concrete measures to prevent modification or loss of data (suspension of circular backups, read-only copy, isolation).
  • Spoliation of Evidence — Alteration, destruction, concealment, or improper withholding of relevant evidence; can be intentional or negligent; has serious legal consequences.
  • E-Discovery — Process of identifying, collecting, reviewing, analyzing, and producing electronic information in response to a legal request.
  • EDRM — Electronic Discovery Reference Model; structured in phases (Information Governance → Identification → Preservation → Collection → Processing → Review → Analysis → Production → Presentation).
  • Order of Volatility — Principle requiring that the most volatile data be acquired first: CPU cache → RAM → Swap → Network connections → Processes → Temporary files → Disk → Remote backups.
  • Write Blocker — Device or function that prevents writes on a medium during forensic acquisition; mandatory to avoid altering the original.
  • Chain of Custody — Continuous documentary record of who has had access to evidence, when, and for what purpose; guarantees admissibility in legal proceedings.
  • Bit-by-Bit Copy — Forensic sector-by-sector copy of the entire medium (including unallocated spaces and deleted files); verified via hash; forensic gold standard.
  • Forensic hash — MD5/SHA-256 value calculated on the original and the copy; if they match, integrity is demonstrated; basis of digital evidence admissibility.
  • Admissibility — Requirement making evidence usable in court; requires verified integrity, documented chain of custody, accepted acquisition method.
  • Firewall Logs — Record permitted/blocked connections, IPs, ports, protocols; useful for perimeter traffic; do not see internal east-west traffic.
  • OS-Specific Security Logs — OS audit logs; on Windows the Security Event Log (Event ID 4624, 4625, 4720, 4688); primary source for authentications and account abuse.
  • Metadata — Data about data: MACE timestamps, email headers, GPS coordinates in images; can be modified (timestomping) by attackers.
  • Packet Capture (PCAP) — Complete dump of network packets including headers and payload; maximum granularity; limited by volume and TLS encryption.
  • NetFlow / Network Logs — Network conversation metadata (who, when, how much) without payload; scalable to high traffic; useful for detecting lateral movement.
  • Vulnerability Scan — Snapshot of system security state; in a post-incident investigation, comparing with previous scans reveals changes made by the attacker.
  • SPAN Port / Network TAP — Mechanisms for capturing network traffic: SPAN replicates via switch (software), TAP replicates via inline hardware device (more reliable for forensics).
  • Cloud Forensics — Forensic acquisition in the cloud depends on contracts (audit clause), provider APIs, jurisdiction; impossible to directly access physical hardware.
